A Personal Tribute

For Kabuli Rath

Debanshu Rath · 1963 – 2026
Guitarist · Composer · Music Arranger

He grew up in a government housing colony in Bhubaneswar — Type VA 16/1, Unit 2, Bhubaneswar, Orissa, 751009 — a neighbourhood of civil servants and their families, the ordinary streets of a modest Indian city in the 1960s and 70s. He was a native of Puri, born to Gangadhar Rath and Prabhavati Rath — herself a classical vocalist — and music surrounded him from the beginning. His grandfather Khetra Mohan Kar was a tabla player. His uncle was the renowned Odia singer Prafulla Kar. Music was not something Debanshu Rath chose. It chose him by inheritance, and he spent his life honouring that inheritance completely.

He and his brothers formed The Brothers — arguably Orissa's first rock band, active from the 1970s through the late 1980s — and heard in the blues and rock and roll something that resonated with everything the Indian classical tradition had already given him. He understood, as few musicians do, that the blues and the Indian classical forms are not opposites. They are the same human need expressed through different histories. That understanding shaped everything he made.

Over a career spanning nearly four decades, he contributed to iconic soundtracks across every major Indian film industry — from A R Rahman's celebrated Roja with its delicate reggae to Imtiaz Ali's heavy metal riffs in Rockstar, the exquisite guitar jugalbandi with shehnai master Pandit Ballesh in The Dichotomy of Fame, and the viral Kolaveri Di. His wide-ranging distinctive style left its mark on compositions by legends like A R Rahman and Ilaiyaraaja, across Bollywood, Tollywood, Kollywood, Mollywood, and Odia cinema. In Odia films alone he worked as background music director and arranger on over 80 films, collaborating with music directors including Akshay Mohanty and Prafulla Kar. He was based in Virugambakkam, Chennai, where for over twenty years he taught guitar — in person and online — passing the tradition to the next generation of musicians exactly as it had been passed to him.

He died in March 2026 at the age of 62. This compendium was built in his memory. It is an attempt to put into words and music what he already knew — that the blues is not a style, it is a truth, and truth does not require a passport or an address. It travels from the Mississippi Delta to a housing colony in Bhubaneswar and back. It travels anywhere two people who love music sit together and listen.

— DR. M. KRISHNA PRASAD MULAVISALA · HYDERABAD, 2026

Chapter One

The Delta Soil

Before electricity. Before Chicago. Before anything — just a man, an acoustic guitar, and the unmediated truth of the Mississippi Delta.

The blues was born in the Deep South of America in the late 19th and early 20th century — among the children and grandchildren of slaves, in the cotton fields and juke joints and churches of Mississippi, Alabama and Texas. It was not invented. It emerged — from field hollers, from work songs, from African musical traditions carried across the Middle Passage and transformed on American soil.

The men in this chapter recorded on primitive equipment in the 1920s and 1930s. Some of these recordings sound ancient, dusty, barely audible. Listen anyway. The truth in them is permanent.

Charley Patton
c.1891 – 1934
The Father of the Delta Blues

Everything begins here. Charley Patton grew up on Dockery Plantation in Mississippi and became the most influential musician nobody outside the blues world has ever heard of. His voice was enormous — gravelly, raw, almost unintelligible — and he played his guitar percussively, slapping and beating it as much as strumming. He was also a showman: throwing the guitar, playing it behind his back, decades before Jimi Hendrix made those moves famous.

Son House learned from Patton. Robert Johnson learned from Son House. Muddy Waters learned from Robert Johnson. The entire Delta lineage — and therefore the entire history of rock and roll — flows from this one man on a Mississippi plantation.

Pony Blues (1929)
The recording is over ninety years old and sounds like it. But press through the surface noise and hear what is underneath — that voice like gravel and thunder, that percussive guitar, something ancient and completely alive. This is where the river starts.

Blind Lemon Jefferson
c.1893 – 1929
The First Great Blues Recording Star

Born blind in Texas, Jefferson was one of the first blues musicians to record commercially, from 1926 onwards. That high, keening, almost unearthly voice and his extraordinary guitar work — complex, ornate, completely self-taught — set him apart from everything around him. He influenced virtually every blues guitarist who followed. BB King cited him directly. He sold hundreds of thousands of records at a time when that was almost unimaginable.

He died alone in a Chicago snowstorm in 1929. He was perhaps 35 years old.

Matchbox Blues (1927)
That guitar work is extraordinary — listen to how complex and ornate it is for 1927. This song was later recorded by Carl Perkins, then the Beatles. The chain from a blind Texas musician in 1927 to the Beatles in 1964 is direct and unbroken.

Lead Belly
c.1888 – 1949
The King of the Twelve-String Guitar

Huddie Ledbetter — Lead Belly — played a twelve-string guitar like nobody before or since. That massive, ringing, resonant sound was entirely his own invention. Twice imprisoned, twice discovered by folk song collectors John and Alan Lomax inside Louisiana and Texas prisons. His repertoire was vast: blues, folk, spirituals, work songs, children's songs. He carried a century of African-American musical tradition in his voice and his twelve strings.

His songs have been recorded by everyone. Kurt Cobain performed Goodnight Irene. The entire folk revival of the 1960s — Bob Dylan, Pete Seeger — runs directly through Lead Belly.

Midnight Special (recorded 1934)
The Midnight Special was a real train — if its light shone through your cell window at Sugarland Prison in Texas, legend said you would go free. That longing for freedom, that train in the distance — this is the emotional heart of the blues.

Son House
1902 – 1988
The Preacher Who Chose the Devil's Music

Son House was a preacher who felt the pull of the blues as a personal moral crisis. His music carries that tension — the church and the juke joint pulling him in opposite directions simultaneously. He learned from Charley Patton and became the direct teacher of Robert Johnson. His slide guitar playing, using the neck of a bottle, produced a sound like crying, like anguish made physical.

He was rediscovered in 1964 by blues researchers who found him living in Rochester, New York, having largely given up music. His late recordings carry the weight of a man who understood he had witnessed the beginning of everything.

Death Letter Blues (1965 version)
A man receives a letter telling him his lover has died. He walks to see her body. The entire song is the journey — that slow, inevitable walk toward loss. His slide guitar sounds like weeping. This is the blues at its most naked and true.

Robert Johnson
1911 – 1938
The Legend at the Crossroads

Robert Johnson recorded only 29 songs between 1936 and 1937. He died at 27 — poisoned, it is believed, by a jealous husband. Those 29 songs changed everything. Eric Clapton called him the most important blues musician who ever lived. Keith Richards said hearing Robert Johnson for the first time was like listening to two guitarists playing simultaneously. Bob Dylan said Johnson had a sound nobody has been able to touch.

The legend says he sold his soul to the devil at a Mississippi crossroads in exchange for his guitar abilities. The legend exists because the music sounds like it might be true.

One man knew Robert Johnson better than almost anyone — Johnny Shines (1915–1992), a Tennessee-born guitarist who met Johnson in 1935 and travelled with him from that year until 1937, accompanying him across the United States and Canada. Shines later said of Johnson: "Robert was about the greatest guitar player I'd ever heard. The things he was doing was things that I'd never heard nobody else do — especially his slide stuff. His guitar seemed to talk." After Johnson's death Shines moved to Chicago, carried Johnson's songs and playing style forward for decades, and became one of the last living authentic Delta blues musicians. He was re-discovered in 1966 by Vanguard Records and recorded for the rest of his life — his final album winning a W.C. Handy Award. One critic wrote that Shines was "the most vigorous surviving practitioner of acoustic Delta blues — with his intense vibrato, his observant, imaginative, tradition-soaked lyrics, and his incomparable slide guitar." He is the living memory of Robert Johnson made flesh. He died in 1992 in Tuscaloosa, Alabama.

Cross Road Blues (1936)
A man stands at a crossroads as darkness falls, trying to flag down a ride, knowing the devil is close. That desperation, that darkness coming — the guitar plays two parts simultaneously, the voice cries between them. Cream made this famous in 1968. The original is more terrifying.

Chapter Two

The Chicago Fire

The Great Migration brought thousands of Black Southerners to Chicago. They brought the Delta blues with them — and plugged it into electricity.

Big Bill Broonzy
1903 – 1958
The Bridge Between Two Worlds

Big Bill Broonzy stood at the crossroads of acoustic country blues and the new urban Chicago sound — absorbing both and transmitting both. He recorded prolifically through the 1930s and 40s, worked with everyone, and became one of the biggest names in Chicago blues. But his most historically significant act was travelling to Europe in the early 1950s when the blues was being pushed aside in America by rock and roll.

European audiences received him as a revelation. Those young British musicians who would become the blues revival — Alexis Korner, Cyril Davies, and through them John Mayall, and through Mayall the young Eric Clapton — heard Broonzy and understood immediately. Without Big Bill Broonzy, there is no British blues revival. There is no Clapton. The lineage is direct.

Key to the Highway (1941)
That rolling, gentle, irresistible groove. The blues of departure — leaving because staying is impossible. This song has been recorded by dozens of artists including Clapton and Allman Brothers. Return always to Broonzy's original and hear where it came from.

Muddy Waters
1913 – 1983
The Father of Chicago Blues

McKinley Morganfield grew up on Stovall Plantation in Mississippi — where Alan Lomax recorded him for the Library of Congress in 1941. He moved to Chicago in 1943, plugged his Delta slide guitar into an amplifier, and invented the Chicago electric blues. That combination of Delta rawness with electric power and a full band — bass, drums, harmonica — created a sound that was entirely new and completely irresistible.

The Rolling Stones took their name from his song. He is why they exist. He is why Clapton exists. He is why Led Zeppelin exists. The entire rock and roll era flows from this one man's decision to electrify the Delta blues on the South Side of Chicago.

Mannish Boy (1955)
That riff. That swagger. That voice. Barely two chords and a vocal boast — but the confidence, the absolute authority, the groove that refuses to resolve. This is the Chicago electric blues at its most elemental. Everything rock and roll ever claimed about itself starts here.

Howlin' Wolf & Hubert Sumlin
Wolf: 1910–1976 · Sumlin: 1931–2011
The Voice from the Earth and the Guitar Beneath It

Chester Burnett — Howlin' Wolf — was six feet three inches tall and weighed three hundred pounds. His voice came from somewhere geological, not human. He learned from Charley Patton and carried that Delta rawness all the way to Chess Records in Chicago. There has never been a voice like his in any music anywhere.

Hubert Sumlin was his guitarist for decades — one of the most influential and least celebrated guitarists in blues history. That angular, unexpected, almost abstract style: notes placed where you didn't expect them, rhythms that seemed to defy convention. Jimi Hendrix cited him. Keith Richards cited him. Eric Clapton paid for his funeral when Sumlin died in 2011. Wolf without Sumlin is incomplete. They were elemental together.

Smokestack Lightning (1956)
A slave in the fields sees the smoke and lightning flash of a distant locomotive — something that could carry him home but will never stop for him. That wail — that cosmic howl — is not a musical choice. It is the sound of that longing made audible. The riff never resolves. It circles like smoke. There is no other song like it.

Elmore James
1918 – 1963
The King of the Slide Guitar

Elmore James took Robert Johnson's slide guitar vocabulary and electrified it into something that could break a room in half. That bottleneck slide technique — a glass or metal tube on the finger, bending strings to produce a sound uncannily close to the human voice — became his signature. He influenced every slide guitarist who followed: Duane Allman, Derek Trucks, Bonnie Raitt, all of them.

He died young — a heart attack at 45 — but the recordings he left are permanent.

The Sky Is Crying (1959)
A love song — the whole world weeping in sympathy with a man whose lover has left him. Those opening notes of slide guitar are among the most immediately recognisable in blues history. Stevie Ray Vaughan named his posthumous album after this song. The original remains the definitive version.

Sonny Boy Williamson II
c.1912 – 1965
The Delta Trickster

Rice Miller borrowed the name of another, more famous harmonica player — an audacious act of identity theft that somehow he got away with, because his talent was undeniable. Born in the Mississippi Delta, he played with Robert Johnson, recorded for Chess Records, and then toured Britain in the 1960s where he completely astonished the young musicians who would become the British blues revival — the Animals, the Yardbirds, a young Eric Clapton. They were in awe.

He reportedly said of the British bands with magnificent dry contempt: "These young men would like to play the blues very much, but they cannot quite manage it." The confidence of a man who knew exactly what he was.

Help Me (1963)
That harmonica — deep, breathing, completely authoritative. The blues harp is the most intimate of all instruments. It is played with breath, with life itself. Williamson's playing goes in and out of his body. When he bends a note it feels like something being exhaled from somewhere very old and very true.

Chapter Three

The Three Kings

BB, Albert and Freddie — no relation by blood, everything in common by music. Between them they define the entire emotional range of the electric blues guitar. But first: the man who made all three of them possible.

T-Bone Walker
1910 – 1975
He Invented the Electric Blues Guitar · Everything That Came After Came From Him

Aaron Thibeaux Walker was born in Linden, Texas in 1910 and raised in Dallas. His stepfather taught him guitar, banjo, mandolin, violin and piano. Blind Lemon Jefferson — the founding father of Texas blues — was a family friend who came to dinner. The young T-Bone guided Jefferson around Dallas for his gigs, absorbing the older man's technique by proximity. He was a professional performer by fifteen. He made his first recordings in 1929. He was nineteen years old.

What he did between then and 1947 was invent a new instrument. He moved to Los Angeles in the mid-1930s, picked up an electric guitar, and understood something that nobody else had grasped yet — that electricity was not just amplification, it was transformation. By using the amplifier's volume to sustain notes, by combining that sustain with single-string bending and finger vibrato, he created a guitar voice that could go head to head with brass and woodwinds as a genuine solo instrument. The guitar had been rhythm. T-Bone Walker made it sing. Robert Palmer wrote that he "in effect invented a new instrument."

In 1947 he recorded Call It Stormy Monday — his masterpiece, and one of the most covered songs in the entire blues canon. The chord sequence, the phrasing, the economy, the way each note breathes — it is the template for virtually every slow blues recorded in the next seventy years. BB King heard it and wrote in his autobiography: "I thought Jesus Himself had returned to earth playing electric guitar. T-Bone's blues filled my insides with joy and good feeling. I became his disciple. And remain so today. My biggest musical debt is to T-Bone." Albert King said Walker was the sound he was looking for. Buddy Guy studied him. Chuck Berry named him as a primary influence. Jimi Hendrix copied his trick of playing guitar with his teeth. The showmanship, the duck walk, the playing behind the head — all of it traces to T-Bone Walker in the 1940s.

He died on 16 March 1975 in Los Angeles, aged 64. He was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1987. He deserves to be spoken of in the same breath as Muddy Waters and Howlin' Wolf — and he almost never is. That omission says more about how we tell the story of the blues than it does about T-Bone Walker.

In the 1930s, before T-Bone Walker had made a single significant recording, he and Charlie Christian — who would go on to revolutionise the role of the electric guitar in jazz — had a street act together in Dallas combining guitar, dancing and showmanship. Two young men in Texas simultaneously discovering that the electric guitar was capable of something entirely new. Christian died of tuberculosis in 1941 at 25. Walker lived long enough to prove what they had both understood. Between them they changed every form of popular music that followed.
Call It Stormy Monday (But Tuesday Is Just as Bad) — 1947
The masterpiece. That guitar tone — warm, sustained, singing like a human voice. Those chord changes that every blues musician has used ever since. The phrasing that sounds utterly unhurried and carries the full weight of everything. This is the recording that BB King called a religious experience. It is the centre of the electric blues tradition. Everything before it was prologue. Everything after it was response.

Johnny "Guitar" Watson
1935 – 1996
Houston to Space · T-Bone's Heir · The Gangster of Love

John Watson was born in Houston, Texas in 1935 and grew up hearing T-Bone Walker. He was so besotted with Walker's playing that he bought a guitar and taught himself to replicate every nuance of it. Then he left T-Bone Walker behind and became entirely himself — which is how all great blues musicians honour their teachers. He was eighteen years old when he recorded Space Guitar in 1954. It is a recording so far ahead of its time — guitar sustain and feedback used as deliberate musical tools — that producers for decades assumed it had been recorded in the 1960s. It was 1954. Jimi Hendrix would not begin making records for another twelve years.

Frank Zappa cited him as a primary influence. Buddy Guy said he was one of the greatest guitarists he ever heard. BB King acknowledged him. His nickname the Gangster of Love became a Steve Miller song. He bridged T-Bone Walker's Texas blues through 1950s R&B and into the funk and soul era — remaining a live force of extraordinary energy through the 1970s and 1980s. He died on stage in Japan in 1996, mid-performance. He was 61. There is a blues argument that says that is exactly how a musician of his particular ferocity should go — playing, not stopping.

Space Guitar (1954)
Recorded in 1954. Listen to the sustain, the feedback, the way the guitar seems to be bending space rather than simply playing notes. This is not supposed to exist yet. The electric guitar was not supposed to sound like this until the mid-1960s. Johnny Guitar Watson arrived a decade early, played what he heard in his head, and went home. Jimi Hendrix was thirteen years old when this was recorded.

BB King
1925 – 2015
The Most Elegant · He Named His Guitar Lucille

Riley B. King grew up picking cotton on a Mississippi plantation and became the most celebrated blues musician in the world. His vibrato — that shimmering, singing trembling of a note — is the most imitated technique in all of guitar playing. His philosophy was economy: one note placed perfectly is worth a thousand played carelessly. What he did not play was as important as what he did.

He named every guitar he owned Lucille — after a woman who caused a fight at a dance hall where a fire broke out, and BB ran back into the burning building to rescue his guitar. Carlos Santana said BB King was his greatest influence. Clapton said he made him nervous. Every major guitarist of the last sixty years has passed through his influence.

The Thrill Is Gone (1969)
The definitive BB King performance. That opening guitar phrase — four notes that say everything before the voice arrives. The production is lush, orchestral — strings behind the blues — but underneath it all is that vibrato, those bends, that absolute emotional precision. Listen for the space between notes. That silence is where he lives.

Freddie King
1934 – 1976
The Most Powerful · The Texas Cannonball

Freddie King is the guitarist's guitarist. The musicians know. Where BB King is warm and elegant, Freddie King comes at the guitar like he has something to prove and no time to waste. That attacking tone — steel fingerpick and plastic thumb pick producing a bright, cutting, urgent sound. He didn't ask permission. Born in Gilmer, Texas, he merged the Texas and Chicago blues styles into something that had a more contemporary, aggressive edge than anything around him.

Clapton learned his instrumental Hideaway note for note as a young man. Peter Green worshipped him. Stevie Ray Vaughan was shaped by him. He died of heart failure at just 42 — gone far too early from a music that needed him. He was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2012 by ZZ Top.

Going Down (1971)
That descending riff — powerful, relentless, joyful in its aggression. Seven minutes of pure momentum. This song has been recorded by dozens of artists from Joe Satriani to Shemekia Copeland, performed live by guitar giants at jam sessions worldwide. Always return to Freddie King's original. The Texas Cannonball at full velocity.

Albert King
1923 – 1992
The Most Dangerous · Upside Down and His Own Way

Albert King was left-handed, played a right-handed guitar upside down, without restringing it, in his own tuning that nobody else has fully decoded. This is why his bends go the opposite direction from every other guitarist — and why his sound is unlike anything else. That dark, menacing, deeply physical guitar tone. He plays like a force of nature rather than a musician.

Stevie Ray Vaughan worshipped Albert King above all others. Sat at his feet literally. They recorded together in 1983 — In Session — one of the most extraordinary documents in blues history: the young lion and the old master, completely equal in the room. Jimi Hendrix had Albert King in his DNA. Clapton had him. Every serious guitarist does.

Born Under a Bad Sign (1967)
Written by Booker T. Jones and William Bell for Stax Records. Albert King's recording became the definitive version — that slow, heavy, inescapable groove, and that guitar bending against it like something trying to escape. Cream covered it the following year. The riff has appeared in hundreds of songs since. Start with Albert's original.

Chapter Four

The British Disciples

In the 1960s, young British musicians heard American blues records and were changed forever. Their devotion gave the music a second life — and a worldwide audience.

John Mayall & The Bluesbreakers
1933 – 2024
The Most Important Blues School in History

John Mayall was less a performer than a curator and educator — a man absolutely devoted to authentic American blues who created a school disguised as a band. The musicians who passed through the Bluesbreakers read like a who's-who of British rock history. Eric Clapton joined after leaving the Yardbirds. Peter Green replaced Clapton. Mick Taylor replaced Green — and later joined the Rolling Stones. Jack Bruce went with Clapton to form Cream. Each one left carrying the blues education Mayall had given them.

Mayall himself performed and recorded into his eighties, still devoted, still paying homage to the source. He died on 22 July 2024 at the age of 90 — inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame that same year, months before his death. BB King said of him: "If it weren't for him and other British musicians putting their own spin on the blues, a lot of us Black musicians in America would still be catchin' the hell that we caught long before." That is the measure of what he built.

All Your Love (Bluesbreakers with Clapton, 1966)
The Beano Album — named because Clapton was reading a Beano comic during the photo session. This recording captures Clapton's complete arrival as a blues guitarist. That tone, that feel, that absolute authority from a 21-year-old. London walls were graffitied with CLAPTON IS GOD. This is why.

Eric Clapton
b. 1945
Slowhand · The Living Bridge

A boy from Surrey who heard Robert Johnson and Muddy Waters as a teenager and dedicated his entire life to understanding and transmitting that tradition. Clapton is not a blues originator — he is the most devoted and gifted student the music ever produced. Through Cream, Derek and the Dominos, and decades of solo work, he brought the blues to audiences who had never imagined it. His tone — that liquid, singing, sustaining Stratocaster or Les Paul sound — is among the most recognisable in all of recorded music.

He dedicated his Royal Albert Hall concert in 2015 to BB King, who had just died. A man at seventy acknowledging everything he owed. That humility is itself a blues quality.

Peter Green
1946 – 2020
The One Who Made Clapton Nervous

Peter Green replaced Clapton in the Bluesbreakers — an almost impossible act to follow, which he managed by being equally gifted but differently so. Where Clapton was technically precise and emotionally contained, Green was more nakedly feeling, more instinctively bluesy. BB King once said he was the only white man who gave him the cold sweats. Clapton said Green was the only guitarist who ever made him nervous.

He founded the original Fleetwood Mac — a raw, deep blues band before it became a pop group — and wrote Black Magic Woman, which Santana transformed into one of the most famous songs in Latin rock. Green battled severe mental illness for decades. He died in 2020 having made some of the most heartfelt blues recordings the British Isles ever produced.

Black Magic Woman (Fleetwood Mac, 1968)
Hear Peter Green's original before you hear Santana's famous version. Green's is darker, more mysterious, deeply blues. Then hear what Santana did to it — wrapped it in Latin fire, gave it a completely different life. One song, two masterpieces. The blues transmitting across cultures.

Gary Moore
1952 – 2011
Belfast to the Delta · Peter Green's Guitar · Still Got the Blues

Robert William Gary Moore was born in Belfast in 1952. As a teenager in the mid-1960s he saw John Mayall's Bluesbreakers perform in his city and his direction was set. He heard Peter Green play and understood immediately. He spent his life chasing that tone — warm, singing, slightly out of phase — and came closer to it than almost anyone except Green himself. As an extraordinary piece of musical history, Gary Moore eventually acquired Peter Green's actual 1959 Gibson Les Paul — the very instrument on which the Beano Album was recorded, the guitar Jimi Hendrix and Jeff Beck had also played — and used it as his main guitar for years before eventually selling it. Metallica's Kirk Hammett bought it in 2014 for just under half a million dollars.

Moore spent his career oscillating between blues and hard rock — Skid Row in Dublin, Thin Lizzy with Phil Lynott, solo hard rock albums through the 1980s — but the blues was always where he actually lived. In 1990, on his eighth solo album, he stopped pretending otherwise. Still Got the Blues featured Albert King and Albert Collins as guests and George Harrison on one track. It sold three million copies worldwide. It was the most successful album of his career. It introduced an entire generation of rock guitarists to the blues tradition, which was exactly what John Mayall had been doing twenty-five years earlier. The lineage was direct and intentional.

His vibrato was his signature — an aggressive, almost violent vibrato that could crack notes apart and reassemble them as pure feeling. Albert King taught him the value of leaving space. He absorbed it. His tone, especially on Still Got the Blues, is one of the great electric guitar sounds of the recording era — that Les Paul through a Marshall, everything on ten, pulled back to the edge of clean and then pushed just over.

He died on 6 February 2011 from a heart attack while on holiday in Estepona, Spain. He was 58 — taken too soon, as the blues has always taken them. Eric Clapton performed Still Got the Blues in concert as a tribute and later recorded it on his 2013 album Old Sock. Bob Geldof said: "Without question, he was one of the great Irish bluesmen." BB King, Buddy Guy, Albert King, Albert Collins — they all played with him and acknowledged him as one of their own. That acknowledgement is the highest credential a guitarist who was not born in Mississippi can receive.

In 1995 Gary Moore released Blues For Greeny — an entire album of Peter Green compositions, played on Peter Green's actual guitar, as a tribute to the man who had inspired him as a teenager in Belfast. It is one of the most purely devotional acts in all of blues music. A man spending a year of his life honouring his teacher, using the teacher's own instrument, playing the teacher's own songs. The blues transmits itself exactly this way — from hand to hand, from instrument to instrument, across decades.

Buddy Guy
b. 1936
Eric Clapton Called Him the Best Guitar Player Alive · He Was Right

George "Buddy" Guy was born in 1936 on a sharecropper's plantation in Lettsworth, Louisiana, made his first guitar at age seven from wood, hairpins and screen wire, taught himself to play by listening to John Lee Hooker on the radio, and arrived in Chicago in 1957 with nothing but his instrument and his playing. Muddy Waters discovered him. Jimi Hendrix used to attend his concerts and tape them. Eric Clapton called him in print the greatest guitar player alive. When you have the reverence of those three men simultaneously you are operating at a level that almost nobody reaches.

Chess Records signed him but refused to record him playing as he actually played. Leonard Chess told him his live style was "just making noise." So Guy played sessions — on Muddy Waters's records, Howlin' Wolf, Little Walter, Sonny Boy Williamson, Koko Taylor — holding back the full ferocity for the stage. The Chess recordings from 1960 to 1967 are brilliant despite the constraints. Stone Crazy. First Time I Met the Blues. The blues drilled to its most concentrated essence.

His style was unlike anything before — high energy, enormous dynamic range, sudden drops from full power to a whisper and back, a 150-foot guitar cable so he could walk through the audience while playing, physical theatre built around genuine musical genius. He influenced generations of guitarists including Eric Clapton, Jimi Hendrix, Jimmy Page, Keith Richards, Stevie Ray Vaughan, Jeff Beck, Gary Clark Jr. and John Mayer. He is a direct link — the last surviving one — from the original Chicago blues world to the present day.

His 1991 comeback album Damn Right I've Got the Blues — produced after Clapton invited him to join his 24 Nights all-star blues series at the Royal Albert Hall in 1990 — gave him the worldwide recognition he had been denied for thirty years. He has been a constant presence at the Crossroads Guitar Festival. He has been at the Mahindra Blues Festival in Mumbai. At 88 years old, having completed his Farewell Tour in 2024, he still holds court at his own club on South Wabash Avenue in Chicago — Buddy Guy's Legends — occasionally stepping on stage to remind everyone how it sounds when the real thing plays.

Muddy Waters's last words to him: "Don't come out here, I'm doing all right. Just keep the damn blues alive." Buddy Guy has kept it alive for sixty-seven years. He is still keeping it alive.

Jimi Hendrix told people that Buddy Guy was the best guitarist he had ever seen. He came to Guy's Chicago performances repeatedly and recorded them on a tape machine. When Hendrix himself became a star, he said he was trying to do to the electric guitar what Buddy Guy had already done. That testimony — from the man who redefined what a guitar could do — is the most extraordinary endorsement in blues history.

Ranked 78th in Rolling Stone's 100 Greatest Guitar Songs of All Time. This is the restrained version of Buddy Guy. The live versions, where the cable uncoils and the music takes over completely, are where the full force arrives.

Ry Cooder
b. 1947
The Most Eloquent Slide Guitar Alive · Keeper of Every Flame · The Man Who Heard Everything

Ryland Peter Cooder was born in Los Angeles in 1947. At age four his father taught him chords. By eight he was playing folk songs from his parents' record collection. At twelve he heard Blind Willie Johnson's Dark Was the Night, Cold Was the Ground — one of the strangest and most haunting recordings in American music — and his direction was set. He spent the rest of his adolescence tracking down every pre-war blues, gospel, and roots recording he could find, absorbing Reverend Gary Davis, Mississippi John Hurt, and Skip James the way other children absorbed pop radio.

By his late teens he was performing on the Los Angeles blues circuit, had formed the Rising Sons with Taj Mahal, and played in Captain Beefheart's Magic Band. By twenty-one he was one of the most sought-after session guitarists in California. He played on the Rolling Stones' Let It Bleed and contributed the slide guitar solo to Sister Morphine on Sticky Fingers. More significantly — and more quietly — it was Cooder who showed Keith Richards the open G tuning. Richards ran with it. The result was Honky Tonk Women, Brown Sugar, Street Fighting Man, and the entire riff-based vocabulary that defines the Stones' sound. Cooder received no credit. He did not ask for any.

Throughout the 1970s he released a series of albums that amount to the most sustained single-handed excavation of American roots music ever attempted by one musician — country blues, gospel, folk, Tex-Mex, Hawaiian slack key, Dixieland jazz, 1950s R&B — each one different, each one built around material he had rescued from obscurity and reanimated with his own playing. His influence on every subsequent roots guitarist is incalculable and almost entirely unacknowledged in public. The musicians know.

His film work produced two of the greatest scores in cinema history. Paris, Texas (1984) — Wim Wenders's desolate, beautiful film about a man walking out of the desert — is scored entirely with Cooder's slide guitar, built on Blind Willie Johnson's Dark Was the Night. The music and the images are inseparable. You cannot hear one without seeing the other. Then Crossroads (1986), where he played every guitar note attributed to Ralph Macchio's character and wrote the music that makes the film worth watching. The guitar duel at the end — Cooder's Delta blues against Steve Vai's shred — is the whole argument of the blues made audible in four minutes.

In 1997 he went to Havana, gathered a group of elderly Cuban musicians who had been forgotten for decades, and produced Buena Vista Social Club. The album sold millions. It was nominated for an Academy Award as a documentary. It launched the careers of Compay Segundo, Ibrahim Ferrer and Omara Portuondo to worldwide audiences in their eighties. It cost Cooder a $100,000 fine from the US State Department for breaching the Cuba embargo. He paid it without apparent regret.

Rolling Stone called him the most eloquent and beautiful player of the slide guitar alive. Director Walter Hill, who made seven films with him, called him the most talented person he had ever known — not simply a singer or guitarist or folklorist or collector of indigenous musics, but a great artist who uses all these things to make the material of his art.

Ry Cooder learned techniques from Reverend Gary Davis — the Harlem street-corner gospel guitarist who taught Stefan Grossman, who taught a generation of folk-blues guitarists. Cooder was one of the very few to go directly to the source. Gary Davis told him things about the tradition that no recording had captured. That transmission — from an old blind man on a Harlem pavement to a teenager from Los Angeles — is exactly how the blues has always moved. Not through institutions. Through hands.

Close your eyes and you see the Texas desert. Open them and you see a man who has been walking for years and does not remember why. That is what a guitar can do.

John Lee Hooker
1917 – 2001
The Boogie Man · Detroit's Blues · One Chord, All Night

John Lee Hooker was born near Clarksdale, Mississippi in 1917. As a child, Charlie Patton visited the house to see his stepfather. He learned guitar from his stepfather William Moore — a blues musician of a droning, one-chord style entirely distinct from the typical Delta blues — and that inheritance shaped everything. Hooker left home at fourteen, drifted through Memphis and Cincinnati, arrived in Detroit in 1943 to work as a janitor in the Ford Rouge Plant and played clubs by night. In 1948 he recorded Boogie Chillen in one take — stomping on a wooden pallet in time with his guitar, voice, and the relentless one-chord groove. It became the best-selling race record of 1949 and he quit his factory job the next morning.

His style was unlike anything else in the blues. No standard beat. No conventional chord changes. Just that hypnotic, driving boogie — modal, trance-like, elemental — that made every other musician uncomfortable because they could not find where to come in. He rarely played with a band in the early years because nobody could follow his musical vagaries. He recorded under eleven different pseudonyms to evade his recording contract. He was one of the most prolific and the most confusing discographies in blues history.

Buddy Guy named him as an early influence. Jimi Hendrix cited him. The Rolling Stones, The Animals, The Yardbirds, Van Morrison all covered him. Eric Clapton played with him. At 72, he recorded The Healer with Carlos Santana, Bonnie Raitt, and Los Lobos — the biggest album of his career. At 80 he was still going strong. He appeared as a street musician in The Blues Brothers film playing Boom Boom. He appeared in The Color Purple. He had a star on the Hollywood Walk of Fame. He won a Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award in 2000, a week before his death.

As a boy, Charley Patton — the Father of the Delta Blues, the man who taught Son House, who taught Robert Johnson — visited the house to see John Lee Hooker's stepfather. That single biographical fact places Hooker in direct living contact with the very oldest root of the entire tradition. He carried that root from the Mississippi cotton fields to the Detroit factory floor to the stages of the world.
Boogie Chillen (1948)
Recorded in one take. Just him, his electric guitar, and his foot stomping on a wooden pallet. That insistent one-chord drive. That voice — half spoken, completely hypnotic. The best-selling race record of 1949. Everything Canned Heat ever played came from this. Everything ZZ Top ever played came from this. The boogie as a force of nature.

The Queens

The Women Who Built the Blues

They were there before Robert Johnson. Before Muddy Waters. Before any of the men history chose to remember. The women came first.

The history of the blues as usually told is a male story. It should not be. The first commercially recorded blues artists were women. The women who performed in the tent shows and juke joints and vaudeville theatres of the early 20th century created the blues as a recorded form before any man had a chance to. Ma Rainey was recording in 1923. Robert Johnson did not record until 1936. The women came thirteen years before the men history celebrates.

They sang about love and loss and independence and sexuality with a freedom that was extraordinary for their time — and they did it while Black women in America had almost no rights, no protections and no safety. The blues was their language when no other language was available. It remains the most honest music ever made.

Memphis Minnie
1897 – 1973
Guitar Queen · She Beat Muddy Waters and Big Bill Broonzy in a Contest · She Still Won

Lizzie Douglas was born in 1897, ran away to Beale Street in Memphis at thirteen, spent years playing on street corners, joined the Ringling Brothers Circus as a travelling musician, and by the 1920s was one of the most formidable guitar players in the South — male or female. A Columbia Records talent scout heard her playing for dimes outside a Memphis barbershop in 1929 and brought her to New York. Her first recording, Bumble Bee, was an immediate hit. The label gave her the name Memphis Minnie. She kept it the rest of her life.

In the 1930s she moved to Chicago and entered its competitive blues world with complete authority. She organised Blue Monday shows. She won guitar contests. Big Bill Broonzy, in his autobiography, described the night Memphis Minnie beat him in a contest — the jury of fellow musicians awarded her a bottle of whiskey and a bottle of gin. Broonzy grabbed the whiskey and took it under a table. Minnie stood on the shoulders of the judges.

She recorded over 200 songs across thirty years — country blues, urban blues, electric blues — adapting with every era while remaining completely herself. When the Levee Breaks, later made famous by Led Zeppelin, was her song. Me and My Chauffeur Blues. Hoodoo Lady. She played electric guitar on stage in Chicago at least one year before Muddy Waters. No recording exists to prove it. The musicians who were there remember it.

Bonnie Raitt paid for her headstone. The inscription: "The hundreds of sides Minnie recorded are the perfect material to teach us about the blues. For the blues are at once general, and particular, speaking for millions, but in a highly singular, individual voice."

Me and My Chauffeur Blues (1941)

One of the first recordings that connects the country blues tradition directly to the Chicago electric sound that followed. This is the link between Ma Rainey and Muddy Waters. It sounds like nothing else.

Sister Rosetta Tharpe
1915 – 1973
The Godmother of Rock and Roll · She Did It All First · Nobody Paid Attention

Rosetta Nubin was born in Cotton Plant, Arkansas in 1915. She was playing guitar by age four. By six she was touring churches with her mother. By the time she signed with Decca Records in 1938 she was already a fully formed musical force — a guitarist of extraordinary technique and power, a singer of complete authority, a performer with stage presence that nobody who saw her ever forgot.

She was among the first popular recording artists to use heavy distortion on her electric guitar, opening the way to the rise of electric blues. She did this in 1938. Jimi Hendrix was born in 1942. Everything people credit Hendrix with doing to the guitar — using distortion as an expressive tool, pushing amplifiers to their limits, making the electric guitar sound like something that had never existed before — Sister Rosetta Tharpe was doing it fifteen years earlier, in gospel clubs, in front of audiences who thought they were coming to church.

Strange Things Happening Every Day, recorded in 1944, is considered by many historians to be the first rock and roll record. It reached number two on the Billboard chart. She was performing rock and roll a decade before anyone called it that. As she herself said in 1957: "All this new stuff they call rock 'n' roll, why, I've been playing that for years now."

Her guitar-playing technique had a profound influence on the development of British blues in the 1960s. Her European tour with Muddy Waters in 1964, with a stop in Manchester on May 7, is cited by British guitarists such as Eric Clapton, Jeff Beck, and Keith Richards. She played on a platform at an abandoned train station in Manchester for a Granada Television special. Young British guitarists watching that footage understood immediately what she was doing and spent the next decade trying to replicate it. They mostly failed.

Rolling Stone named Tharpe the sixth-greatest guitarist of all time in 2023. She died in 1973 in an unmarked grave in Philadelphia. The men she influenced became legends. The woman who showed them the way was forgotten for decades. She was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2018 — as an Early Influence. Sue Foley released a tribute to her in 2024. The correction is underway, but slowly.

Little Richard was twelve. Elvis Presley was ten. None of them had picked up a guitar yet. Sister Rosetta Tharpe was already here.

Bessie Smith
c.1894 – 1937
The Empress of the Blues · The Highest-Paid Black Entertainer of Her Day

Bessie Smith was orphaned at nine years old in Chattanooga, Tennessee, singing on street corners with her siblings to survive. She joined Ma Rainey's travelling show as a teenager, learned her craft, developed her voice — and by the 1920s had become the most popular blues singer in America and Columbia Records' biggest-selling artist. She rarely used amplification. She didn't need it. Janis Joplin credited her as the reason she wanted to sing. Aretha Franklin was shaped by her. Billie Holiday. She died at 43 after a car accident in Mississippi. There are persistent reports that she was denied treatment at a segregated hospital. Whatever the exact circumstances, the blues and the cruelty of the world she sang about met in one terrible moment on a country road in Mississippi in 1937.

Big Mama Thornton
1926 – 1984
She Recorded Hound Dog Before Elvis. She Wrote Ball and Chain Before Janis.

Willie Mae Thornton — Big Mama — recorded Hound Dog in 1952, three years before Elvis Presley made it a worldwide phenomenon. Her version sold over half a million copies. The story of Big Mama Thornton is in many ways the story of how the blues was taken from its originators without credit or compensation.

She also wrote Ball and Chain — the song that made Janis Joplin famous at Monterey in 1967. Thornton watched from the audience as Joplin performed it and cried. She was not credited. She was not paid.

Despite all of this she remained completely unbroken — performing with energy and authority right to the end of her life, dressing as she pleased, playing as she pleased, acknowledging no authority over her art or her person. She was inducted into the Blues Hall of Fame in 1984. She is still not in the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, despite having invented two of its most celebrated songs.

Lucille Bogan
1897 – 1948
The Most Uncompromising Voice in the Blues · She Said What Nobody Else Would

Lucille Bogan — also recorded as Bessie Jackson — was one of the earliest and most ferocious blues vocalists of the classic era. She recorded from 1923, placing her alongside Ma Rainey and Bessie Smith at the absolute founding of recorded blues. But where Ma Rainey had theatrical grandeur and Bessie Smith had imperial authority, Bogan had something rawer and more dangerous — a complete indifference to what anyone thought of her, a willingness to sing about exactly what she experienced with exactly the language she chose, and the blues chops to make it land every single time.

Her 1935 recording of Shave 'Em Dry is the most explicit recording made in the twentieth century until well past the mid-century. Decades before rock and roll, decades before anyone talked about censorship in popular music, Lucille Bogan said things on record that would not be said again on record for thirty years. She said them with a grin and a guitar and a complete mastery of her craft. The recording was pressed in extremely limited quantities — too hot for regular distribution — and is now a document of extraordinary historical importance: proof that the women of the classic blues era were not polite, were not demure, and were not singing what anyone told them to sing.

She stopped recording in 1935. She died in 1948 in relative obscurity. The music survived her and will outlast everything placed above it.

This is what the classic blues era sounded like when the musicians involved had no intention of being respectable. Then seek out her deeper recordings when you are ready for the full force of what she actually was.

Etta James stood at the crossroads of blues, soul and gospel and made all three her own simultaneously. She could whisper and destroy you. Koko Taylor
1928 – 2009
The Queen of the Blues · Chicago Electric Blues in the Female Voice

Koko Taylor was the daughter of a Tennessee sharecropper who moved to Chicago with her husband in 1953 and changed the face of Chicago electric blues. Her voice was enormous — rough, raw, absolutely authoritative — and she wielded it like a weapon and a celebration simultaneously. Her final performance was at the Blues Music Awards on May 7, 2009, just weeks before her death. She performed right to the end because the music was not something she did — it was something she was.

Barbara Lynn
b. 1942
The Empress of Gulf Coast Soul · Left-Handed · Ahead of Everyone

Barbara Lynn Ozen was born in Beaumont, Texas in 1942. She switched from piano to guitar as a child, taught herself to play left-handed and upside-down without restringing — a technique that produced a sound and attack entirely her own. She formed an all-female band as a teenager. In 1962, at nineteen years old, she walked into Cosimo Matassa's studio in New Orleans with session musicians including Mac Rebennack — later Dr. John — and recorded You'll Lose a Good Thing. It went to number one on the R&B chart and number eight on the pop chart.

What was extraordinary was not just the song — it was everything surrounding it. Unusual for the time, Lynn was a female African American singer who both wrote most of her own songs and played a lead instrument. She was a left-handed guitarist writing her own material and leading her own band in 1962. That was genuinely almost unheard of. Aretha Franklin covered You'll Lose a Good Thing. The Rolling Stones covered Oh Baby on their 1965 album. Otis Redding toured with her. She was at the Apollo Theatre. She was on American Bandstand twice.

She largely retired through the 1970s and 1980s to raise her family, returned in the 1990s, and has been performing and recording ever since. In 2026 Lynn was inducted into the Blues Hall of Fame. She still resides in Beaumont, Texas. The city named a street after her.

You'll Lose a Good Thing (1962)
The original. Written by her, played by her, a number one R&B hit at nineteen years old. That left-handed guitar, that voice, that complete self-possession. Aretha covered it. The Stones covered her other songs. But nobody touched this one. This is Barbara Lynn's entirely.

Sue Foley
b. 1968
The Ice Queen · Pink Telecaster · PhD in Musicology · Still the Best

Sue Foley was born in Ottawa, Canada in 1968 — not the usual origin story for a Texas blues guitarist. She heard James Cotton play live at fifteen and the decision was made instantly and irrevocably. By sixteen she was performing in Ottawa clubs. By twenty-one she had relocated to Austin, Texas, signed with Antone's Records — the label that had been home to Stevie Ray Vaughan — and recorded her debut album Young Girl Blues. She has been one of the finest blues guitarists alive ever since.

Her playing is built on Texas blues — T-Bone Walker, Clarence Gatemouth Brown, Lightnin' Hopkins — delivered through a signature pink paisley Fender Telecaster with painted fingernails and pinpoint precision. She has shared stages with BB King, Buddy Guy, Jimmie Vaughan and Billy Gibbons. She has won the Blues Music Award for Traditional Blues Female Artist five consecutive years from 2020 to 2025.

She also holds a PhD in Musicology from York University and is writing a book about pioneering women guitarists. She is simultaneously the finest practitioner and the most serious scholar of the tradition she inhabits.

The Soul Connection

Where Blues Became Soul

The blues and gospel met on a Saturday night and produced soul music. Otis Redding was its greatest voice.

Soul music is the blues wearing its Sunday best. The same emotional truth, the same roots in African American experience and the church and the cotton fields — but channelled through gospel's transcendence and given a more polished, orchestrated setting. The blues says: I am suffering. Soul says: I am suffering, and I am reaching for something higher. Both are necessary. Both are true.

Otis Redding stood at that exact intersection — blues, gospel, soul, rock — and moved between them in a single performance with complete ease. He is the reason this section exists in a blues compendium.

Otis Redding
1941 – 1967
The King of Soul · Gone at 26 · Permanent

Otis Ray Redding Jr. was born in Dawson, Georgia, the son of a sharecropper who occasionally preached in local churches. He grew up in Macon, Georgia, listening to Sam Cooke and Little Richard, singing in the Baptist church choir, winning a local talent show fifteen weeks in a row. He dropped out of school at fifteen to pursue music, drove a band called the Pinetoppers to a Stax Records session in Memphis in 1962, and — with leftover studio time at the end of the session — recorded These Arms of Mine. His career had begun.

What followed in five years was extraordinary. He recorded at Stax with Steve Cropper of Booker T. and the MGs — one of the great musical partnerships in soul history — and produced a body of work that remains unsurpassed. He wrote Respect in 1965. Aretha Franklin recorded it in 1967 and made it a cultural landmark. He wrote I've Been Loving You Too Long, Mr. Pitiful, I Can't Turn You Loose. He covered the Rolling Stones' Satisfaction and made it sound like his song. He performed at Monterey in June 1967 — the only soul act on a bill dominated by rock — wearing a shiny blue suit and pouring every ounce of himself into the performance. He told the crowd: "This is the love crowd, right?" They roared back.

On December 6, 1967 he recorded Sittin' On the Dock of the Bay — a song unlike anything he had made before, wistful and reflective and completely beautiful. Four days later his plane crashed into Lake Monona in Madison, Wisconsin. He was 26 years old. The song was released three months after his death and became his only number one single.

Rolling Stone ranked him 21st among the 100 Greatest Artists of All Time and 8th among the 100 Greatest Singers. Q magazine ranked him fourth among all singers in history — after only Sinatra, Franklin and Presley. Five of his albums appear on Rolling Stone's 500 Greatest Albums list. He received the Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award in 1999. And he had been gone for thirty-two years by then. Imagine what he might have done.

The Bee Gees' Barry and Robin Gibb wrote To Love Somebody specifically for Otis Redding to record. He loved it and planned to record it on his return from his final concert. He never came back. They dedicated the song to his memory. Somewhere in that unrealised recording is a glimpse of what the next chapter might have contained.
"This is the love crowd, right?" — Otis Redding at Monterey, June 1967

That building, that release, that moment when he stops holding anything back. Ranked among Rolling Stone's 500 Greatest Songs of All Time. Watch the live version at Monterey for the full experience.

Amy Winehouse
1983 – 2011
The Last Voice That Told the Complete Truth · Camden Town · The 27 Club

Amy Jade Winehouse was born in Enfield, London in 1983 and grew up in a household saturated with jazz — her father hummed Frank Sinatra, her grandmother had been a cabaret singer, and the records that surrounded her childhood were Ella Fitzgerald, Sarah Vaughan, Tony Bennett. She joined the National Youth Jazz Orchestra as a teenager. Jazz was her first language. But what she made from it was something older and more urgent — something that traced back through soul, through rhythm and blues, through the girl groups of the early 1960s, and underneath all of it, to the blues.

Her second album, Back to Black (2006), is one of the great blues records of the twenty-first century. Not because it sounds like Robert Johnson. Because it does what Robert Johnson did — it takes a private agony and makes it so precise, so honestly expressed, that it becomes universal. She understood the tradition the way a great blues singer does: as a vocabulary, not a costume. She was not imitating the past. She was inhabiting it. The Dap-Kings — Sharon Jones's Brooklyn band, steeped in the deepest soul tradition — played behind her on the record. Mark Ronson built the production around Phil Spector's Wall of Sound and the Shangri-Las. Underneath all of it: the blues. The same bones.

She won five Grammys in a single night in 2008. She died on 23 July 2011 at the age of 27. Robert Johnson was 27. Jimi Hendrix was 27. Janis Joplin was 27. Kurt Cobain was 27. The 27 Club is not a coincidence. It is the blues making its oldest argument — that the most honest voices sometimes cannot survive the weight of what they are carrying. Amy Winehouse carried a great deal. She carried it in public, with extraordinary dignity and without self-pity, and put it all into two albums. Two albums. That is the complete work. It is enough.

In 2025, Back to Black was selected by the Library of Congress for preservation in the National Recording Registry as "culturally, historically, or aesthetically significant." Robert Johnson's recordings were selected in 1998. The Library of Congress recognises what the music press sometimes misses — that the blues is not a genre. It is a form of testimony. Amy Winehouse testified.
Back to Black (2006)
The title track. A funeral in three minutes and forty-two seconds. That voice — contralto, warm, devastating — over the Dap-Kings' rhythm section and a tolling bell. The whole album is the gateway but this song is its centre. Close your eyes and you hear exactly what it costs to tell the truth about yourself in music. The blues has always known what that costs. Amy Winehouse knew too.

Janis Joplin
1943 – 1970
Port Arthur, Texas · Bessie Smith's Heir · The Voice That Tore Itself Apart for You

Janis Lyn Joplin was born on 19 January 1943 in Port Arthur, Texas — a Gulf Coast oil town, conservative, segregated, not a place that rewarded difference. She was different from the beginning. Bullied through high school for her appearance, her opinions, her complete refusal to be what Port Arthur wanted her to be. She found her people in a small circle of outcasts who collected jazz and blues records. Through them she discovered Bessie Smith, Ma Rainey, Big Mama Thornton and Lead Belly. That discovery, she later said, gave her the decision to become a singer. It gave her something deeper — a language for everything she was carrying that had nowhere else to go.

She did not imitate those women. She absorbed them the way a great blues musician absorbs their influences — taking specific characteristics and making them entirely her own. From Big Mama Thornton she took the refusal to sound polished, the attack, the gravel, the total force. From Bessie Smith she took the ability to dominate a room, to stretch a phrase until it broke and hold it together anyway. From Ma Rainey she took theatrical swagger and vaudeville-blues looseness. She pushed her voice until it cracked and frayed and scraped. She pushed it because the blues demands everything you have and she gave everything she had every single time.

Her Monterey Pop Festival performance in 1967 — singing Big Mama Thornton's Ball and Chain with Big Brother and the Holding Company — is one of the great moments in American music. She was 24. She came offstage and the world had changed. Cheap Thrills (1968) went to number one. Piece of My Heart. Summertime. Woodstock in 1969. And then Pearl, recorded in autumn 1970 — Me and Bobby McGee, Mercedes Benz, Cry Baby. She died on 4 October 1970 in a Hollywood hotel room. Heroin overdose. She was 27.

She paid for Bessie Smith's headstone. When Joplin learned that the greatest blues singer who had ever lived was lying in an unmarked grave in Philadelphia, she arranged for a stone to be erected at Smith's grave in Sharon Hill, Pennsylvania, on 7 August 1970. Joplin died exactly 58 days later. The epitaph reads: "The Greatest Blues Singer in the World Will Never Stop Singing." Bonnie Raitt paid for Memphis Minnie's headstone. The Queens take care of each other across decades. The tradition is real and it runs in both directions through time.

Janis Joplin appeared on the cover of Newsweek in May 1969 next to the headline "Rebirth of the Blues." She had been bumped off the cover a month earlier by the death of President Eisenhower. That juxtaposition contains something true about America in 1969 — the old world and the new one running on parallel tracks, the blues outlasting everything placed above it.
Ball and Chain — Monterey Pop Festival, June 1967
Big Mama Thornton's song. Janis Joplin's performance. Watch the film of this — not just listen, watch. Watch her face. Watch what it costs. Watch Mama Cass Elliott in the audience, mouthing "wow" in complete disbelief at what she is witnessing. This is what the blues looks like when a 24-year-old tears herself open in public and 50,000 people understand they are seeing something that will never happen again exactly this way. The blues. Derek Trucks
b. 1979
The Primordial Scream of the Guitar

Perhaps the most gifted slide guitarist alive. Playing professionally at thirteen, joining the Allman Brothers Band at nineteen. Robert Cray
b. 1953

Robert Cray was born in Columbus, Georgia in 1953, grew up as an Army brat moving from state to state, and settled in Tacoma, Washington as a teenager. Chapter Eight

The Blues in India

The music that emerged from the Mississippi Delta has taken root in the Indian subcontinent — finding voices that carry its truth with complete authenticity.

The blues arrived in India through vinyl records, cassettes, and the ears of young musicians in the 1960s and 70s who heard something in it that resonated deeply. This was not accidental. The blues and Indian classical music share fundamental structural DNA — the bending of notes, the microtones, the modal scales, the call and response, the emotional directness, the insistence on feeling over technical display. Indian musicians often take to the blues instinctively because something in the musical vocabulary is already familiar.

The Northeast in particular — Shillong, Meghalaya, the Darjeeling hills — has produced a disproportionate number of serious blues musicians. Something in that culture, that Anglo-Indian musical heritage, that hill country sensibility carries the music naturally. What follows is not a complete survey — it is an introduction to the artists who are doing the real thing.

The True Wail

The Blues Harp

The harmonica — the instrument that fits in a shirt pocket and sounds like the entire human soul crying at once.

It costs almost nothing. It weighs almost nothing. You can carry it in your pocket from the Mississippi Delta to Chicago to London and back. And in the right hands it produces the most nakedly human sound in all of blues music — a sound that bends, cries, wails, laughs and groans with a directness that no other instrument can match. The harmonica sounds like a voice because it is played with the breath, the tongue, the lips, the whole body. It is the most intimate instrument in the blues.

The blues harp players are among the least celebrated figures in the tradition — overshadowed by the guitarists, underpaid, under-recorded. But without them the whole sound of Chicago blues would be incomplete. They were the voice inside the voice. In blues harmonica, there are only two eras: before Little Walter and after Little Walter. That is how large he stands.

Sonny Boy Williamson I
John Lee Williamson · 1914 – 1948
The First Great Blues Harp Star · He Invented the Vocabulary

John Lee Williamson — Sonny Boy Williamson I — was the first blues harmonica player to achieve genuine stardom as a recording artist. From 1937 to 1947 he recorded over a hundred sides for Bluebird Records and established the harmonica as a lead instrument in the blues band. His style was loose, conversational, deeply vocal — the harp as a second voice answering the first. Good Morning School Girl. Stop Breaking Down. Sugar Mama. Every one of these songs entered the permanent blues vocabulary.

He was murdered on his way home from a Chicago club on June 1, 1948 — robbed and beaten, dying before help arrived. He was 34 years old. His influence on every blues harmonica player who followed him — Little Walter, Sonny Boy Williamson II, Junior Wells, James Cotton — was foundational and total.

Good Morning School Girl (1937)
His debut recording. That conversational harmonica — voice and instrument in such close dialogue you cannot always tell where one ends and the other begins. This is where the blues harmonica as we know it begins. Every player after him built on what he laid down here.

Sonny Boy Williamson II
Rice Miller · c.1912 – 1965
The Trickster · The Greatest Blues Performer on Stage

Aleck Ford Rice Miller — Sonny Boy Williamson II — had the audacity to steal the name of an already-famous harmonica player and make it more famous than the original. He performed under the alias of the deceased John Lee Williamson for years, eventually eclipsing the original in legend and reputation. His harmonica playing was completely unlike anyone before or after him — rough, eccentric, full of spaces and surprises, conversational in a way that sounded like a man arguing with himself and winning.

By the time of his death in 1965 he had bookended the entire blues era — playing with Robert Johnson early in his career and recording with the Yardbirds — including a young Eric Clapton, Jimmy Page and Brian Jones — at its end. That is the span of the blues tradition made flesh in one man. His European recordings with the Animals and the Yardbirds are among the most important documents of the blues reaching British ears.

Bring It On Home. Help Me. Nine Below Zero. Eyesight to the Blind. King Bee. Every song a drama, every performance a performance — the man was born for the stage and he knew it. He dressed extravagantly, told tall tales, was impossible and magnificent and completely irreplaceable.

Sonny Boy Williamson II told people he had taught Robert Johnson to play harmonica. He told people he was born in 1897. He told people he had invented the blues. None of it was true. But such was his presence, his authority, his absolute confidence in his own legend, that people believed him. The blues was always partly theatre — and Rice Miller was its greatest actor.

His masterpiece. That slow, hypnotic groove, that voice like gravel and smoke, and the harmonica weaving through it all like a second consciousness. Chess Records at its finest. The Rolling Stones covered it. Led Zeppelin covered it. Everyone covered it. None of them could touch the original.

Little Walter
Marion Walter Jacobs · 1930 – 1968
The Greatest · Before and After — That Is All There Is

There is a line in blues harmonica history that every serious player acknowledges: before Little Walter and after Little Walter. On one side of that line the harmonica was a folk instrument. On the other side it was a fully amplified lead instrument capable of matching an electric guitar note for note, phrase for phrase, with tone and expressiveness that had never been heard from any instrument before.

Marion Walter Jacobs was born in Marksville, Louisiana in 1930, abandoned by his mother, raised on a farm. He taught himself harmonica at eight years old. By twelve he had quit school and was travelling America busking. By eighteen he was on Maxwell Street in Chicago, already the most ferocious player anyone had heard. By twenty-two he had recorded Juke — the only harmonica instrumental ever to reach number one on the Billboard R&B chart — and left Muddy Waters's band to lead his own.

What he did with amplification was extraordinary. Where other players used the microphone merely for volume, Little Walter pushed his amplifiers to distortion, cupped the harmonica and microphone together in his hands to create feedback and resonance effects, and produced sounds — sweetly sustained moans, razor sharp howls, thundering chords — that had never existed before. He had done what Jimi Hendrix would do with the guitar fifteen years later: made the amplifier part of the instrument.

His harmonica on the classic Muddy Waters recordings — Hoochie Coochie Man, I'm Ready, Mannish Boy — is as essential as Muddy's slide guitar. Without Little Walter those records are different records. He had fourteen top ten R&B hits between 1952 and 1958. He influenced every harmonica player who came after him — Junior Wells, James Cotton, Paul Butterfield, Charlie Musselwhite, Mick Jagger, Brian Jones, Jerry Portnoy. His biographers say simply: he began expanding the vocabulary of the blues harmonica with an approach like a jazz horn soloist — setting the harmonica free, granting it equality with every other instrument.

He died at 37 after a street fight in Chicago. Alcoholism and violence had been his companions since adolescence. His face, by the time of his death, was a roadmap of scars. He had been inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2008 — forty years after his death. The Hall called Juke one of the 500 Songs That Shaped Rock and Roll. It was the only harmonica instrumental on the list.

"There have been many great players, but only two distinct eras: pre-Little Walter and post-Little Walter." — Tony Glover, blues historian

That amplified tone — warm, flexible, swinging like a saxophone — announcing something that had never existed before. This is the moment the blues harmonica became a lead instrument. Everything after this is after this.

Big Walter Horton
1921 – 1981
Willie Dixon's Favourite · The Most Beautiful Tone in Blues Harmonica

Willie Dixon — the man who wrote more blues standards than almost anyone — called Big Walter Horton the greatest harmonica player he ever heard. Robert Palmer named him alongside Little Walter and Sonny Boy Williamson II as one of the three great harmonica soloists of modern blues. And yet Horton remains the least known of the three — a quiet, shy, reclusive man who never sought the spotlight and lived in near-poverty for much of his life.

His tone was extraordinary. Where Little Walter was jagged, urgent, saxophone-like, Horton was fluid, horn-like, almost orchestral — a sound of enormous warmth and spatial intelligence. He played with Muddy Waters, Jimmy Rogers, Otis Rush, Johnny Shines. His harmonica work on Otis Rush's I Can't Quit You Baby — later a Led Zeppelin song — is one of the most beautiful things in all of blues. He gave lessons to both Little Walter and James Cotton in the 1940s, making him the teacher of the next generation even as his own career remained in the shadows.

Paul Butterfield
1942 – 1987
The Bridge to the Rock Generation · Newport 1965 · He Brought Chicago Blues to White Ears

Paul Butterfield grew up on Chicago's South Side in the 1950s and did something almost no white teenager was doing — he walked into the Black blues clubs on the West and South Side and asked to sit in. Muddy Waters let him. Howlin' Wolf let him. Little Walter taught him. Big Walter Horton befriended him. By his late teens he was playing blues harmonica at a level of authenticity that the Black masters themselves acknowledged. He absorbed Big Walter Horton's tone, Little Walter's chromatic technique, Junior Wells's vocal phrasing, and synthesised them into a voice that was entirely his own — and then he took that voice to the white rock and folk audience that had never heard what Chicago blues really sounded like at source.

The Paul Butterfield Blues Band's 1965 debut album — with Mike Bloomfield and Elvin Bishop on guitars, Jerome Arnold on bass, Sam Lay on drums — was the first racially integrated electric blues band to achieve mainstream recognition in America. It was also simply one of the great blues albums. Then on 25 July 1965 the Butterfield band backed Bob Dylan at the Newport Folk Festival when Dylan went electric. That performance — Maggie's Farm at full Chicago blues volume in a folk festival — is one of the most written-about moments in American cultural history. Butterfield was 22 years old. He was already the most important blues harmonica player of his generation.

In 1966 he recorded East-West — a landmark album that featured the 13-minute title track, an extended blues-raga improvisation that predated the psychedelic era and pointed the way toward jazz-rock fusion. He performed at Woodstock in 1969. He appeared in The Last Waltz, Martin Scorsese's 1978 film of the Band's farewell concert. He died on 4 May 1987 from peritonitis brought on by long-term drug abuse. He was 44. Muddy Waters, who had let a white teenager sit in with him thirty years earlier, knew what he had built.

Chapter Eleven · Part II

The Architects

The guitar gets the glory. The piano, bass and drums built the house. Without these musicians there is no Chicago blues, no Chess Records, no rock and roll. Here are the people who made the sound.

Every great blues recording you have ever heard rests on a foundation that rarely gets named. The guitarist takes the solo. The singer gets the credit. But behind them — holding the whole structure together, creating the groove that makes everything else possible — are the pianist, the bassist, and the drummer. In the Chicago blues era especially, these musicians were often the same people on every session: Otis Spann on piano, Willie Dixon on bass, Fred Below on drums. They were the engine of Chess Records. They were the sound of Chicago. They made it all possible.

Otis Spann
1924 – 1970
The Greatest Post-War Blues Pianist · The Soul of the Muddy Waters Band

Otis Spann was born in Belzoni, Mississippi and began playing piano at seven years old, taught by a local pianist named Friday Ford whom he adored. He moved to Chicago in 1946, was mentored by the great Big Maceo Merriweather, and in 1952 became the piano player in Muddy Waters's band — a position he would hold for sixteen years. What followed was one of the great partnerships in blues history. Spann played on Hoochie Coochie Man, I'm Ready, Got My Mojo Working — the very foundation stones of the Chicago blues sound. His rolling left hand, his crashing treble fills, his deep understanding of space and timing gave the Muddy Waters band its emotional centre.

He was in constant demand at Chess Records as a session pianist — playing on sessions with Bo Diddley, Howlin' Wolf, Sonny Boy Williamson, Little Walter, and Buddy Guy. He recorded with Peter Green and Fleetwood Mac. He recorded a solo album for Decca UK with Muddy Waters and a young Eric Clapton. The Village Voice called him "the greatest modern blues pianist." The Ann Arbor Blues and Jazz Festival named its performance area the Otis Spann Memorial Field after his death. He died of liver cancer in Chicago in 1970 aged 46 — another one taken far too soon. His grave was unmarked for almost thirty years before a headstone was finally erected. Even in death the blues gives its greatest servants the hardest road.

Memphis Slim

John Len Chatman was born in Memphis in 1915, took the name Memphis Slim, and over the course of a fifty-year career recorded more than 500 songs — making him one of the most prolific figures in blues history. Professor Longhair
1918 – 1980
The Father of New Orleans R&B Piano · Fess · The Rhumba-Blues

Henry Roeland Byrd — Professor Longhair, universally known as Fess — is the founding father of New Orleans R&B piano. Born in 1918, he developed a style that blended blues, boogie-woogie, rumba and second-line New Orleans rhythms into something entirely his own and entirely original. Dr. John said that without Professor Longhair there is no New Orleans music as we know it. Allen Toussaint called him "the Bach of rock and roll." His playing had a rolling, syncopated, polyrhythmic quality that sounded unlike anything else in American music — both deeply rooted in the blues and pointing toward something new. He was discovered late, rediscovered after retirement, and finally played the first New Orleans Jazz and Heritage Festival in 1971 where his performance was the defining moment of the event. He died in 1980 just as he was receiving the recognition he had always deserved. His influence on every New Orleans musician who followed is total and permanent.

Willie Dixon
1915 – 1992
The Poet Laureate of the Blues · Chess Records · 500 Songs · He Wrote the Blues

Willie James Dixon was born in Vicksburg, Mississippi in 1915 and came to Chicago during the Depression. He was a heavyweight boxing champion of Illinois before he became a musician — which may explain his complete refusal to be pushed around by anyone, including the Chess brothers. He joined Chess Records in 1951 as house bassist, arranger, producer and staff songwriter. Next to Muddy Waters, Dixon is recognised as the most influential person in shaping the post-war sound of Chicago blues.

The songs Dixon wrote during his Chess years are the canon. Hoochie Coochie Man — Muddy Waters. Little Red Rooster — Howlin' Wolf. Back Door Man — Howlin' Wolf. Spoonful — Howlin' Wolf. My Babe — Little Walter. Wang Dang Doodle — Koko Taylor. I Can't Quit You Baby — Otis Rush. You Can't Judge a Book by the Cover — Bo Diddley. I Just Want to Make Love to You — Muddy Waters, then the Rolling Stones, then Foghat. Whole Lotta Love — Led Zeppelin, who did not initially credit Dixon and paid settlement after a lawsuit. He wrote over 500 songs. He defined what Chess Records sounded like. He defined what Chicago blues sounded like. He defined what rock and roll sounded like — because all three are inseparable.

In later life he founded the Blues Heaven Foundation, fighting to recover copyrights and royalties for blues musicians who had been exploited — including himself. He said: "The blues are the roots and the other musics are the fruits. It's better keeping the roots alive, because it means better fruits from now on." He died in 1992. The Blues Heaven Foundation purchased the former Chess Records building in Chicago in 1997 in his memory. It stands there now.

Fred Below
1926 – 1988
He Invented the Chicago Blues Beat · Chess Records · Johnny B. Goode

Frederick Below Jr. was born in Chicago in 1926, trained as a jazz drummer at the Roy C. Knapp School of Percussion under the influence of Buddy Rich and Gene Krupa, and arrived back in the city after Army service to find that jazz was no longer the music of choice — blues was. According to musicologist Tony Russell, Below was a creator of much of the rhythmic structure of Chicago blues, especially its backbeat. He is in short one of the most important musicians in the history of American popular music, and almost nobody knows his name.

He joined The Aces — Junior Wells, Louis and Dave Myers — and then became Little Walter's drummer when Walter commandeered the band in 1952. In 1955 he became the staff drummer at Chess Records. What followed was a cascade of recordings that created the sound of an era: Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf, Elmore James, Otis Rush, Buddy Guy, Junior Wells — and then Chuck Berry. He was the drummer on Chuck Berry's Johnny B. Goode. He was the drummer on Bo Diddley's debut. He was the drummer on Muddy Waters's most important recordings. He played on the Moonglows' Sincerely. He played on records by the Platters and Dinah Washington. The Chicago blues shuffle — that rolling backbeat with the jazz feel — is his invention. As he said himself: "The blues, if played correctly, is not easy to master. People who say it's a simple music form never played the blues. If they did, they probably didn't play it right. It's very hard to play somebody's feelings, and that's what the blues is." He played somebody's feelings on hundreds of recordings. He died of liver cancer in Chicago in 1988.

This is what Below invented. Every blues drummer who came after him was working from this template whether they knew it or not. Then listen to Johnny B. Goode and hear the same man creating the backbeat that rock and roll was built on.

Jack Bruce
1943 – 2014
The Bass as Lead Instrument · Cream · Glasgow to the Summit of Rock

John Symon Asher Bruce was born in Bishopbriggs, Lanarkshire, Scotland in 1943. He won a scholarship to study cello and composition at the Royal Scottish Academy of Music, composed a string quartet at eleven years old, and then quit the Academy because his jazz playing was disapproved of. Classical music's loss was the blues's gain. He joined Alexis Korner's Blues Incorporated, then the Graham Bond Organisation, then John Mayall's Bluesbreakers, and in the summer of 1966 formed Cream with Eric Clapton and Ginger Baker. Roger Waters called him "probably the most musically gifted bass player who's ever been."

What Bruce did with the bass had never been done before. Bass had been a rhythm instrument — background, invisible, supportive. In his hands it became a lead voice, melodic and assertive, weaving through the music with the independence of a front-line instrument. Cream took Willie Dixon's Spoonful, Robert Johnson's Crossroads, Albert King's Born Under a Bad Sign and played them with improvisational freedom that introduced those songs to an entire generation — who then went back to the originals. That is the blues transmission working exactly as it should. He co-wrote Sunshine of Your Love, White Room and I Feel Free with lyricist Pete Brown. He influenced Sting, Geddy Lee, Geezer Butler — every rock bassist who came after was working from the template he built. He died of liver cancer on 25 October 2014. Eric Clapton said: "He was a great musician and composer, and a tremendous inspiration to me."

Ginger Baker
1939 – 2019
Rock's First Superstar Drummer · Cream · Africa · Jazz and Blues in Collision

Peter Edward Baker was born in Lewisham, South London in 1939 — nicknamed Ginger for his flaming red hair. He trained as a jazz drummer under Phil Seamen, joined Alexis Korner's Blues Incorporated in the early 1960s, moved to the Graham Bond Organisation, and in 1966 formed Cream. Rolling Stone ranked him third on their list of the 100 greatest drummers of all time. He titled his memoir Hellraiser: The Autobiography of the World's Greatest Drummer — which tells you everything about his personality and not enough about his genius.

Baker said himself: "Oh for god's sake, I've never played rock. Cream was two jazz players and a blues guitarist playing improvised music." That is exactly correct — and it is why Cream's music endures. His polyrhythmic approach, twin bass drums and jazz-trained sense of space brought something to blues-rock that nobody had brought before. He did not play the Chicago shuffle the way Fred Below did. He played over, around and through it — creating a rhythmic conversation that gave Clapton's guitar room to breathe and pushed Bruce's bass into new territory. After Cream he moved to Africa, recorded with Fela Kuti, and absorbed West African polyrhythmic traditions — becoming the first rock-era drummer to seek out and master African drumming in its own environment. Stewart Copeland said: "He personally is what drums are all about." John Bonham, Neil Peart and a generation of rock drummers learned directly from him. He died on 6 October 2019, cantankerous and magnificent to the end.

Then listen to Cream's Spoonful live — sixteen minutes of three-way improvisation where the drums are not keeping time but having a conversation. This is what Ginger Baker brought to the blues tradition.

At the Summit — Two More Names

Steve Gadd (b. 1945, Rochester, New York) is widely considered the greatest session drummer who ever lived. His jazz precision — heard on Paul Simon's 50 Ways to Leave Your Lover, Steely Dan's Aja, Chick Corea, and hundreds of other recordings — represents the summit of what Fred Below started in Chicago in the 1950s. When Gadd plays blues sessions, the feeling is total and the lineage is unmistakable. He is the living proof that the discipline Below built is permanent and transferable.

Nathan East (b. 1955, Philadelphia) is the most recorded bassist in history — Eric Clapton, Michael Jackson, Lionel Richie, Daft Punk, Stevie Wonder, and decades of work with Fourplay. His long collaboration with Eric Clapton on blues recordings, including the Unplugged album and Clapton's later blues work, gives the tradition a contemporary voice of complete authority. Where Jack Bruce made the bass a lead instrument through force, Nathan East makes it indispensable through grace. Both approaches are the blues.

Chapter Twelve

The Blues on Screen

Film found the blues and the blues found film. The greatest movies about this music are pilgrimages — journeys toward something true that cannot be fully explained, only felt.

The blues has produced a body of cinema unlike any other musical tradition. Not just films about musicians — but films that understand the blues as a philosophy, a way of moving through the world. The best of them are not music documentaries. They are road movies, ghost stories, origin myths and comedies of the human condition. What they share is that the music is never decoration. It is the point.

The Blues Brothers
1980 · Dir. John Landis
A Mission from God · The Greatest Blues Concert Film Ever Made, Disguised as a Comedy

Jake and Elwood Blues — John Belushi and Dan Aykroyd — are on a mission from God to save the orphanage they grew up in by putting on a concert. Everything else that happens is elaborate, absurd, and completely irrelevant to why this film is essential. What matters is the music. Ray Charles behind a keyboard in a music shop selling instruments. Aretha Franklin as a diner cook who stops the film cold with Think. James Brown as a preacher. Cab Calloway. John Lee Hooker on a street corner playing Boom Boom. The house band featuring Steve Cropper and Duck Dunn from Booker T. and the MGs — the Stax Records rhythm section — providing the backbone.

The film works for blues purists and for people who have never heard a blues record in their lives. That is its genius. It smuggles an entire education into a car-chase comedy. You watch it laughing and you come out knowing who Ray Charles was, what Aretha Franklin's voice does to a room, and why Boom Boom is one of the greatest riffs in American music. The car chases are extraordinary. The music is better.

John Belushi died in 1982. The sequel in 2000 was everything the original was not. Watch the original and stop there.

Crossroads
1986 · Dir. Walter Hill

All the guitar playing you hear from Eugene is actually Ry Cooder — one of the great roots music guitarists in American history, and the film's composer and musical director. Cooder's soundtrack is extraordinary: classical guitar, Delta blues, Chicago electric blues and Appalachian folk woven together into a coherent musical language. The film uses it perfectly. Every scene is scored to exactly the right piece.

The climax is a guitar duel at a crossroads — the crossroads — where Eugene must defeat the devil's personal guitarist Jack Butler, played by Steve Vai, or Willie loses his soul. What follows is one of the most remarkable scenes in music film history. Ry Cooder begins playing straight Delta blues. Steve Vai responds with everything he has — the full arsenal of 1980s shred guitar at its most brilliant and terrifying. Cooder finds one more note. One classical phrase. And the duel turns. The scene works because it is not really about technique. It is about what the blues actually is — where it came from, what it cost, and why no amount of virtuosity without feeling can touch it.

Ry Cooder's soundtrack was recorded separately from the guitar duel sequence. O Brother, Where Art Thou?
2000 · Dir. Joel & Ethan Coen
Homer's Odyssey · The Great Depression · T-Bone Burnett's Masterpiece

Three escaped convicts — George Clooney, John Turturro, Tim Blake Nelson — make their way through Depression-era Mississippi in a loose retelling of Homer's Odyssey. The Coen Brothers admitted they had never actually read the Odyssey. It does not matter. What matters is the music.

Producer T-Bone Burnett was brought in while the script was still being written. The music was recorded before filming began — an extraordinary decision that meant every scene was shot to already-completed recordings rather than music added in post. The soundtrack was recorded before filming commenced, with music conceived as a major component of the film, not merely background or support. The result is a film where the music and the image breathe together in a way that almost never happens in cinema.

The soundtrack encompasses the full pre-war Southern musical landscape — Delta blues, gospel, bluegrass, country, prison work songs. Its unlikely success sparked a roots-music boom that helped establish a commercial audience for Americana that persisted and grew for years. It won the Grammy for Album of the Year 2002 — the first time a soundtrack of traditional music had ever won. It sold nine million copies. It tripled attendance at bluegrass festivals. A single soundtrack album changed the commercial landscape of American roots music.

Cadillac Records
2008 · Dir. Darnell Martin
Chess Records · Chicago Blues · Muddy, Wolf, Walter, Etta — On Screen Together

Chess Records was the most important blues label in history. From a studio on South Michigan Avenue in Chicago — so small the musicians had to crowd together, producing a natural compression that became its own sound — Leonard Chess and his brother Phil recorded Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf, Little Walter, Willie Dixon, Chuck Berry, Bo Diddley, Etta James and dozens more. They changed music. The Rolling Stones named their own rehearsal studio after the Chess Studios address: 2120 South Michigan Avenue. The address is a song.

Cadillac Records takes considerable dramatic licence — invented romances, compressed timelines, historical events adjusted for narrative convenience. The facts on the ground were complicated enough without invention. Leonard Chess did give his artists Cadillacs instead of royalties — buying luxury cars with the very money he owed them and calling it a gift. Muddy Waters, asked near the end of his life if he resented it, said: "I made them millionaires, they made me a NAME." That single sentence is more complex and more honest than anything in the film.

The film stars Adrien Brody as Leonard Chess, Jeffrey Wright as Muddy Waters, Columbus Short as Little Walter, Cedric the Entertainer as Willie Dixon, Eamonn Walker as Howlin' Wolf, Mos Def as Chuck Berry, and Beyoncé Knowles as Etta James. Jeffrey Wright as Muddy Waters is extraordinary — quiet, powerful, completely true. Eamonn Walker as Howlin' Wolf is genuinely frightening. Beyoncé as Etta James is the performance of her career.

Martin Scorsese Presents the Blues
2003 · PBS · Seven Episodes

In 2003, Martin Scorsese gathered seven of the world's greatest filmmakers and gave each of them a different chapter of the blues story. Scorsese himself directed Feel Like Going Home — a journey through Mississippi with musician Corey Harris, tracing the music all the way back to its West African roots. Wim Wenders directed The Soul of a Man, about Skip James, Blind Willie Johnson and J.B. Lenoir. Clint Eastwood directed Piano Blues, about the pianists who underpinned the entire tradition. Ghost World
2001 · Dir. Terry Zwigoff
Skip James · Devil Got My Woman · The Most Unlikely Blues Film Ever Made

Ghost World is not a blues film. It is a film about two alienated teenage girls — Thora Birch and Scarlett Johansson — adrift in a generic American suburb after high school. But at its centre is Steve Buscemi playing Seymour, a record collector whose obsession is pre-war blues 78 rpm records. The specific record that drives the plot is Skip James's Devil Got My Woman from 1931.

Skip James — Nehemiah Curtis James of Bentonia, Mississippi — recorded a handful of sides in 1931 in a high, keening, otherworldly voice that sounded unlike any other blues. Then he disappeared. He was rediscovered in 1964 in a Tunica hospital, dying, by three white record collectors who had been searching for him for years. He performed at the Newport Folk Festival that same year and was astonished to learn that his recordings had been cherished for three decades by people he had never met. He died in 1969.

Ghost World understands something essential: that old blues records carry an emotional charge that cannot be explained rationally. Seymour is not collecting nostalgia. He is collecting evidence. Evidence that human feeling at its most intense can survive on a piece of shellac for seventy years and still destroy you when you hear it. That is the blues. That is why we are all here.

Chapter Thirteen

The Gathering Places — Concerts & Festivals

The blues was built in juke joints and on street corners — music too honest for concert halls. Then the concert halls came to the blues. These are the gatherings that changed everything.

Blues concerts are not mere entertainment events. They are acts of preservation, transmission and community. Each major festival in this list served a specific historical purpose — rescuing artists from obscurity, introducing a tradition to new audiences, carrying the music across oceans, or simply keeping it alive when the commercial world had moved on. Between them they document 140 years of music reaching forward to find its next listeners.

Newport Folk Festival
1959 – present · Newport, Rhode Island
Where the Blues Met the Folk Revival · Robert Johnson Reaches a New Generation

The Newport Folk Festival, founded by jazz promoter George Wein in 1959, became the most important single venue for the blues reaching white American college audiences in the early 1960s. The 1963 Newport blues workshop — featuring Mississippi John Hurt, Brownie McGhee, Sonny Terry and John Lee Hooker performing together at the Newport Casino — is considered one of the seminal moments of the folk revival. It caused Mississippi John Hurt, who had not been heard from since his 1928 recordings, to suddenly be performing at universities and appearing on The Tonight Show. The blues tradition had been sitting in silence for thirty years and Newport woke it up.

Then came July 25, 1965 — the most famous night in the festival's history. Bob Dylan walked on stage with the Paul Butterfield Blues Band and played electric. He was booed by a small faction of folk purists. The sound was too loud, they said. The blues was too present, too raw. What happened that night was actually the blues asserting itself in the most public possible way — the acoustic folk world and the electric blues world colliding in front of 20,000 people. The shockwave is still audible. Newport has continued ever since, with BB King, Bonnie Raitt, Susan Tedeschi and many others bringing the blues to its stage decade after decade.

Watch — Dylan Goes Electric, Newport 1965
The most analysed moment in the history of popular music. Watch it not for Dylan but for the Butterfield Blues Band behind him — Mike Bloomfield on guitar, Jerome Arnold on bass, Sam Lay on drums. Those are Chicago blues musicians playing at full electric volume in a folk festival. That collision is the moment rock and blues became permanently inseparable.

American Folk Blues Festival
1962 – 1985 · Europe (Germany, UK, Scandinavia)
The Tour That Gave the British Blues Its Roots · Willie Dixon Takes Chicago to Europe

The American Folk Blues Festival was the most important blues event in European history. Conceived by German jazz publicist Joachim-Ernst Berendt and promoted by Horst Lippmann and Fritz Rau, it was organised with the crucial assistance of Willie Dixon, who gave the promoters access to the Chicago blues community. Beginning in 1962 and continuing almost annually through 1972, then reviving from 1980 to 1985, the tour brought the original architects of Chicago blues to European concert halls for the first time.

The lineups were extraordinary — Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf, Willie Dixon, John Lee Hooker, Little Walter, Sonny Boy Williamson, T-Bone Walker, Memphis Slim, Big Mama Thornton, Otis Rush, Junior Wells, all in their prime, performing in Germany and across Europe. The impact on young British musicians was total and permanent. The Rolling Stones, the Animals, the Yardbirds, John Mayall, Eric Clapton, Peter Green — all of them saw these concerts and built their careers from what they witnessed. The festival DVDs contain the only known footage of Little Walter. The tour is the direct ancestor of the entire British blues movement.

Monterey Pop Festival
June 16–18, 1967 · Monterey, California
The Summer of Love's Greatest Weekend · Janis Joplin and Otis Redding Change Everything

The Monterey International Pop Festival of June 1967 was the weekend that the blues moved to the centre of popular culture. Three days of music in the California sun, broadcast to the world and filmed by D.A. Pennebaker. The blues was everywhere — in Otis Redding's devastating closing set on the Saturday night, in Janis Joplin's Ball and Chain that introduced her to the world, in the Paul Butterfield Blues Band's electric Chicago sound, in the Grateful Dead, in the Blues Project. And in Jimi Hendrix — who set fire to his guitar at the end of his set, having synthesised every strand of blues guitar tradition into something entirely new.

Otis Redding's performance on that Saturday night is one of the great concerts in blues and soul history. He had never performed to a predominantly white rock festival audience before. He walked out, looked at 50,000 people, and said "I've been told you're the love crowd — I believe it." Then he played Try a Little Tenderness, Shake, I've Been Loving You Too Long — and the love crowd was destroyed. He died six months later in a plane crash aged 26. Monterey is the only major filmed record of Otis Redding at his absolute peak.

Ann Arbor Blues Festival
1969 · Ann Arbor, Michigan
The First Major Blues Festival · "The Festival of the Year, If Not the Decade"

Two weeks before Woodstock, approximately 20,000 people gathered at the Fuller Flatlands on the banks of the Huron River in Ann Arbor, Michigan, for what was — incredibly — the first major blues festival ever held in the United States. Organised by University of Michigan students Cary Gordon and John Fishel, it assembled what may be the greatest blues lineup ever staged on a single programme: BB King, Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf, Otis Rush, Magic Sam, Big Mama Thornton, Son House, T-Bone Walker, Lightnin' Hopkins, Junior Wells, James Cotton, Charlie Musselwhite, Clifton Chenier, Roosevelt Sykes, J.B. Hutto, Freddie King, Sam Lay — the full canon, alive and performing.

Downbeat critic Dan Morgenstern dismissed Woodstock — which happened two weeks later and drew fifteen times the crowd — in favour of Ann Arbor, calling it "without doubt the festival of the year, if not the decade." Jim O'Neal, co-founder of Living Blues magazine, wrote: "Survivors of the 1960s still ask one another, 'Were you at Woodstock?' Those of us who became blues converts ask, 'Were you at Ann Arbor?'" The 2019 Third Man Records release of previously unheard recordings from the festival debuted at number 10 on the Billboard Blues Albums Chart. The festival site was later renamed the Otis Spann Memorial Field in tribute to the great pianist who died in 1970.

Chicago Blues Festival
1984 – present · Millennium Park, Chicago
The World's Largest Free Blues Festival · Chicago Honours Its Own

The Chicago Blues Festival began in 1984 as a one-time event to honour the recently deceased Muddy Waters, who had died in 1983. It has run every year since, growing into the world's largest free blues festival, drawing over 500,000 attendees annually to Grant Park and later Millennium Park with the glittering Chicago skyline as backdrop. Entry is free. The music is free. The tradition is honoured. It is one of the great acts of civic generosity in American cultural life.

The festival's lineup over the decades reads like the complete history of the blues — BB King, Albert King, Buddy Guy, Koko Taylor, Albert Collins, Etta James, Bo Diddley, Bobby Bland, Charlie Musselwhite, Shemekia Copeland, Gary Clark Jr. Every major figure in the tradition has performed here. The city of Chicago built the blues, and every June it comes back to Millennium Park to remember that fact. The Blues Foundation's annual Blues Music Awards are held in Memphis each year; the Chicago Blues Festival is where the living tradition gathers to perform.

Montreux Blues Festival
Within the Montreux Jazz Festival · 1967 – present · Switzerland
Europe's Most Prestigious Blues Stage · The Lake Geneva Lakeside That Heard Everything

The Montreux Jazz Festival, founded by Claude Nobs in 1967 on the shores of Lake Geneva in Switzerland, has always been as much a blues festival as a jazz festival. The lakeside stage has hosted virtually every significant blues artist of the last sixty years — BB King, Muddy Waters, John Lee Hooker, Buddy Guy, Etta James, Albert Collins, Robert Cray, Stevie Ray Vaughan, Gary Moore, Bonnie Raitt, Dr. John, Professor Longhair, Charlie Musselwhite. The recordings released from Montreux constitute one of the most important archives of live blues performance in existence.

Stevie Ray Vaughan's 1982 Montreux performance — where he was largely unknown, played over the opposition of some in the crowd, and was seen by David Bowie and Jackson Browne who both subsequently contacted him — is one of the great origin stories in blues-rock history. The Montreux recordings of John Lee Hooker, BB King and Etta James are among the finest live documents of those artists. The festival's setting — on the lakeside beneath the Alps — gives the blues a context it never had in its original geography, which somehow makes the universality of the music even more apparent.

Stevie Ray Vaughan — Montreux 1982
The performance that launched SRV. He was unknown, some in the crowd were hostile to electric blues in what they considered a jazz festival, and he played as if the fate of the tradition depended on it. David Bowie was watching from the side of the stage. Jackson Browne was watching. Both called him afterwards. The blues has always found its audience by refusing to be quiet.

Mahindra Blues Festival
2011 – present · Mehboob Studios, Bandra, Mumbai
The Blues Reaches India · The Most Important Blues Festival Outside the West

The Mahindra Blues Festival, inaugurated on 5–6 February 2011 at Mehboob Studios in Mumbai's Bandra neighbourhood, is the most significant blues festival outside North America and Europe — and arguably the most important development in the global blues community in the twenty-first century. Spearheaded by Anand Mahindra and directed by VG Jairaman of Oranjuice Entertainment, it brought world-class blues to India and in doing so proved that the music's reach is genuinely universal.

The first edition was headlined by Buddy Guy, who pledged his support without hesitation and at the end of his two-hour set invited all the other musicians to join him on stage for an impromptu jam — the blues tradition of generous, spontaneous community playing itself out in a Mumbai studio as naturally as it had in any Chicago club sixty years earlier. Subsequent editions brought John Mayall, Walter Trout, Charlie Musselwhite, Shemekia Copeland, Larkin Poe, and the best of India's own blues musicians. In 2020 the festival launched Blues in Schools, teaching harmonica to girls from underprivileged backgrounds. The Mahindra Blues Festival Band Hunt has annually discovered and elevated Indian blues talent.

The festival matters for the same reason this compendium matters — the blues is not a regional music. It travels anywhere two people who love music sit together and listen. The Mahindra Blues Festival proved that Mumbai is as natural a home for this music as Memphis.

Other Significant Blues Gatherings

King Biscuit Blues Festival (Helena, Arkansas, 1986–present) — Named after the legendary King Biscuit Time radio show, the longest-running daily radio programme in the United States. Held every October in the heart of the Delta, on the streets of Helena where Sonny Boy Williamson II broadcast to the region every day. The most geographically authentic major blues festival in existence.

Mississippi Delta Blues & Heritage Festival (Greenville, Mississippi, 1978–present) — The oldest continuously running blues festival in the United States, held in the heart of the Delta where the music was born. Essential pilgrimage.

International Blues Challenge (Memphis, Tennessee, annual) — Run by the Blues Foundation, it is the premier competition for emerging blues talent from around the world. The stage at Beale Street. The tradition judging its own future.

New Orleans Jazz & Heritage Festival (New Orleans, April–May, annual) — While primarily a jazz and roots festival, Jazz Fest has hosted definitive blues performances for fifty years, in the city where the blues and jazz first began their conversation. Professor Longhair's 1971 performance here was the moment that restored his career and his legend.

Notodden Blues Festival (Norway, annual) — Europe's largest dedicated blues festival, held in a small Norwegian town of 13,000 people that somehow transforms itself every August into the blues capital of Europe. The fact that Norway hosts one of the world's great blues festivals is the most eloquent possible proof that this music belongs to everyone.

The Thread Newport 1963 rescued Mississippi John Hurt from thirty years of silence. The American Folk Blues Festival gave the British Invasion its roots. Monterey 1967 showed what Otis Redding and Janis Joplin could do to an audience that had never heard them. Ann Arbor 1969 gathered the complete canon in one place two weeks before Woodstock, and nobody talked about it enough. The Chicago Blues Festival has honoured the tradition freely every June since 1984. Montreux has recorded it for sixty years. And the Mahindra Blues Festival proved in 2011 that Mumbai is as natural a home for this music as Memphis. The blues does not stay in one place. It goes where the truth is needed.
The Last Word

Why the Blues Matters

You have travelled a long road through this compendium. You began in the Mississippi Delta in the early 1900s — with men and women who had almost nothing, who lived under a system designed to crush them, who somehow took a guitar and a voice and made something that the entire world eventually came to hear. You end here, in the present, where young musicians from Georgia and Ottawa and Hyderabad are playing that same music with the same honesty and the same fire.

The question worth asking, at the end of all this, is: why? Why did this music, born in such pain, in such poverty, in such specific and local circumstances — why did it travel so far? Why did it matter to a teenager in Surrey who heard Muddy Waters on a record and wept? Why does it matter to you, reading this, wherever you are?

The answer, I think, is this: the blues is the only musical form that takes human suffering completely seriously without resolving it. Gospel says: the suffering will end. Classical music says: the suffering can be transcended. Pop music says: the suffering can be forgotten. The blues says: the suffering is real, it is here, it has always been here, and we will play it until it becomes bearable. Not because playing makes the suffering go away. But because playing proves you are still alive inside it.

That is why Robert Johnson's recordings still raise the hair on your arms after ninety years. That is why Bessie Smith's voice still makes you ache. That is why you cannot listen to Otis Redding at Monterey — that blue suit, that voice, that audience roaring — without feeling that something permanently true about being human is happening in front of you. The blues speaks to everything that cannot be spoken about any other way.

Son House said it simply: "The blues ain't nothing but a good man feeling bad." But it is also a good woman feeling bad. It is a young musician in an Indian city feeling something ancient and true through their fingers. It is a retired cardiac surgeon in Hyderabad holding a nylon string guitar that was given to him by his closest friend — a friend who played the blues for fifty years in Bhubaneswar and is now gone — and knowing that the right way to honour that gift is to play.

Start anywhere. But start. The blues will meet you exactly where you are.

The Chain Charley Patton taught Son House. Son House taught Robert Johnson and Muddy Waters. Muddy Waters taught Little Walter and Jimmy Rogers and Buddy Guy. BB King taught Eric Clapton. Clapton took it to the world. The world brought it back changed. Memphis Minnie played electric guitar before anyone else did, and Bonnie Raitt paid for her headstone. Sister Rosetta Tharpe invented rock and roll and died in an unmarked grave. The chain holds even when nobody is watching it. It has always held. It will always hold.

"The blues is not a style. It is a truth. And the truth has no expiry date."
